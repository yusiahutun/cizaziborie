Video covers black men's hair fashion trends over the last 100 years

Publish date: 2024-06-10

Black men of America are the latest to receive the 100 Years of Beauty treatment in a gorgeous new video covering everything from the 50s rock pompadour to the 90s flattop.  

After covering homogenized male beauty from the last century in a previous video, the filmmakers at Cut.com have taken on another important facet of American male style over the last 100 years: that of African Americans.

Running less than a minute and a half long, the clip focuses on handsome model and musician Lester Shaw as he is clipped, styled and made up into a variety of stunning looks that encapsulate every decade of the past century - and take particular influence from icons in civil rights.

All elegance: Musician Lester Shaw is the star of Cut.com's latest 100 Years of Beauty series, which covers the last century in African American hairstyles

Clean-cut: For the early part of the century, the filmmakers borrowed from the styles of pioneer aviator William J. Powell and Donald Sheffield Ferguson, the first black medical student at Kansas University

Starting with the 1910s, Lester's hair is covered by a hat, which visual anthropologist Chris Chan explains in another video is done because 'in the early 20th century, most people wouldn't be caught leaving the house without a hat on'.

For the next decade, the filmmakers looked to pioneer aviator and a civil rights activist William J. Powell for inspiration, giving Lester a clean cut look, topped off with a fetching fedora.

Donald Sheffield Ferguson, the first black medical student at Kansas University, provides the look for the 1930s with a hard side parting in his hair, while the 1940s provide the filmmakers with the opportunity to pay tribute to the black men who served in the military during the World Wars by giving Lester a close-cropped hairstyle and an army cap.

Fighting for America: The 1940s gives the filmmakers a chance to pay tribute to the men who fought in the World Wars

Rocking out: For the 1950s, Lester is done up to look just like rock'n'roll pioneer Little Richard (left) with a high-rising conk hairstyle 

Strong look: The next decade's influence came from the Black Panther movement

Leader: Huey Newton, one of the founders of the Black Panther movement, was the specific inspiration for filmmakers 

Given that American rock'n'roll wouldn't exist without the talents of the many black artists in the first half of the century, the 1950s makeover leaves Lester looking the spitting image of rock pioneer Little Richard with the singer's trademark conk hairstyle and thin mustache.

The obvious choice for the 1960s is referencing the Black Panther movement, using images of founder Huey Newton with his Afro and black beret as a model.

Things are a bit more wild and free with the next decade's style, including a picked-out Afro, lengthy sideburns and a goatee - a choice that researcher Chris sees as something that 'completely resists' the discipline that had been forced on black bodies in times prior.   

Superfly: During the 1970s, the picked out Afro was a hit against previous restrictions put on black bodies

Arty: Artist Jean-Michel Basquiat's uniquely styled hair and artistic activism gave Lester his 1980s look

Lovin' it: Lester dons one of the most iconic looks of the 1990s with a perfectly-coiffed flattop

Rocking the look: Will Smith's character on Fresh Prince of Bel-Air not only provided a perfect example of the flattop, but also is considered by the filmmakers to be an 'interesting symbol' of the times

Artist Jean-Michel Basquiat's uniquely styled hair inspires the 1980s look, which Cut.com compare to that of The Weeknd's style today - and top it off with a colorful headband. 

It doesn't get more 90s than the hairstyle chosen by the researchers for Lester: the perfectly coiffed flattop, as sported by Will Smith as the Fresh Prince of Bel-Air.

'The Fresh Prince of Bel-Air is an interesting symbol actually, because in the case of Will Smith's character, you have this person trying to mediate middle-class existence coming from the inner city,' explains Chris.

'You cannot look at Fresh Prince of Bel-Air without thinking in the background about Rodney King and the LAPD incidents and about race riots and urban blight.' 

All on his own: Lester himself was the inspiration for the 2000s style, including a goatee and braids

Of the era: The style is the same as that worn by D'Angelo in the infamous video for his song Untitled (How Does It Feel), which happened to be released on the first day of the millennium 

A combination: The modern-day look takes a version of the flattop accompanied by a fade trim on the side

For the 2000s, Cut.com actually draws from the model's own styles of the decade past, with a neatly-trimmed goatee and his hair in braids stretching over his head, down to the base of his skull - just like D'Angelo in his infamous video for Untitled (How Does It Feel), released coincidentally on the first day of the millennium.

Lester's style for the present day is in tune with the vintage obsessions in American fashion, with a hairstyle that is 'a throwback reference to the flattop, but with the fade,' Chris explains, adding that the style is also popular with white artists such as Macklemore.

The clip is the latest in Cut.com's long series of 100 years of Beauty videos, which have previously covered women from Japan, Russia, Germany and more.
