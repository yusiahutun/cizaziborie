She never hesitated to make the most of her voluptuous figure in their sketches, but at times the Monty Python boys went too far even for Carol Cleveland.

The ‘Female Python’, now 72, was known for her scantily clad roles on Monty Python’s Flying Circus but, when a scene called for her to go topless, she refused.

Miss Cleveland, who appeared in 34 of the show’s 46 episodes, said: ‘There was one sketch in the early days and we were doing location filming on Torquay beach and I was being chased by a man eating desk.

'I was this Marilyn Monroe type and I was dressed in a white fur coat and a lacy dress underneath and a lace bra.

'My bra was supposed to come off after being trapped in a cacti. I was supposed to run past John Cleese and he was supposed to say, “Now for something completely different”.

‘We had a huge crowd of people on the beach and this is early days. I did not fancy running topless in a crowd of people and that was the only time I said “No” to the Pythons, so they filmed me running from the back.’

While they used her looks to great effect, it turns out the Pythons were not immune to Miss Cleveland’s charms, with Terry Gilliam once asking her out.

The actress, who is taking part in next month's Python reunion stage show, said: ‘If I did not have a boyfriend who knows, I might have been Mrs Gilliam. I have read he is the richest. They have all done very well.’

The request to pose topless in Torquay is not the only time in Miss Cleveland’s career people have wanted to make use of her famous assets.

The actress, who also appeared in One-Upmanship and The Lotus Eaters, once auditioned for London’s Windmill Theatre, a venue for variety shows, known for having nude women on stage.

She said: ‘I did not know you had to be totally naked and I went and I was offered the job and when I found out you had to be naked I could not do it.’

The actress, pictured right here with Eric Idle in Monty Python's 'And Now For Something Completely Different', said she refused to go topless during a scene being filmed on Torquay beach

Miss Cleveland, who was married to the actor Peter Brett between 1971 and 1983, has also revealed that before she found fame with the Pythons, she worked as a bunny in Playboy’s Park Lane club.

She said: ‘I thought it would be fun and my Italian boyfriend at the time did not like the idea. It was in London and I lasted six months.

‘I was one of the first Playboy bunnies when it opened in Park Lane. You had to do the bunny dip when you served drinks. If you fell forward the boobs could hit the table first.’

Terry Jones, Eric Idle, Carol Cleveland, Michael Palin, Terry Gilliam and John Cleese rehearse in London for their new show Monty Python Live (mostly) which is on at the O2 Arena in London in July

Monty Python’s Flying Circus was broadcast by the BBC between 1969 and 1974 and consistently appears in lists of the greatest television programmes of all-time, including those put together by the British Film Institute and TIME.

The group, who also made a series of films, last performed together live in 1980 and their last film was The Meaning of Life in 1983.

Despite their success, they became wracked by rows and disagreements, leading to an eventual split. Graham Chapman died from cancer in 1989 aged only 48.

The actress, now 72, starred in 34 of the show's 46 episodes

The five surviving Pythons went on to find great success in their solo careers. Terry Gilliam is now an Oscar-nominated director, John Cleese has starred in a string of hit television shows and films, Michael Palin is a respected travel writer and presenter, Eric Idle has a successful career as a comedian and a composer and Terry Jones has become known as a writer.

Age, it seems though, has not tempered their humour and next month’s reunion show, which reunites the five surviving Pythons - John Cleese, 74, Eric Idle, 71, Michael Palin, 71, Terry Jones, 72, and Gilliam – for their first show since 1980, promises to be the rudest yet.

Miss Cleveland, who is also taking part in the new stage show, said: ‘On top of seeing their comedy heroes, fans are going to be seeing a much bigger show, a much more exciting show. It’s a little bit ruder than it has been in the past.’

Despite the similarities, there are some changes from the heyday on the 1970s, including Miss Cleveland’s role.

She said: ‘We have 20 ensemble boys and girls and 20 beautiful young ladies, I am now playing more character roles and they are paying my roles in the show. It will be emotional.’

She added: ‘Over the last two years I have finally started playing my age. And I am so happy that I am playing these roles. I feel this is a new chapter in my life.’

Fans are already excited for the 10-date run, which starts on July 1, with tickets selling out in only 43.5 seconds.

The three-hour show will contain both new material and see the group recreate some of their best-loved sketches, including The Parrot Sketch, the Argument Sketch and the song Christmas in Heaven from the film Monty Python and the Meaning of Life.

It promises to be very lucrative for the comedians, with Mr Jones joking after the announcement: ‘I’m quite excited about it. I hope it makes us a lot of money. I hope to be able to pay off my mortgage!’
