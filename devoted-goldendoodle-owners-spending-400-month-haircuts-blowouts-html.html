Devoted goldendoodle owners are spending up to $400-a-month for haircuts and BLOWOUTS

Publish date: 2024-02-16

The rise of the goldendoodle has seen grooming costs go to the dogs - with some owners spending up to $400 a month on blowouts for the pooches, whose breed requires special grooming care. 

Establishments like Hollywood's Dogue Spa charge up to $275 for 'poodles and doodles' to get a bath and a haircut. 

They also offer premium services - everything from a Blueberry Facial, teeth brushing, other kinds of grooming and teeth cleaning and as they put it 'extra love and care.'

Joy Claire, a creative agency owner in West Hollywood, told the Wall Street Journal she had no problem paying $250 each month for her 60-pound goldendoodle Penelope Poodlepants. 

'She's my best friend, so it's always in the budget for her,' she said of her dog, who she describes as a 'muppet gorilla' and naturally, has her own Instagram account. 

Joy Claire, a creative agency owner in West Hollywood, told the Wall Street Journal she had no problem paying $250 each month for her 60-pound goldendoodle Penelope Poodlepants (pictured)

Goldendoodles require daily brushings and a comb to keep their coat from getting matted which can cause ear infections.  

According to pet-sitting service Rover, goldendoodles were the second-most popular puppy breed in 2022. Bernedoodles and labradoodles were also in the top 20. 

A 2023 survey by Rover deemed goldendoodles the most expensive breed to care for.

Joshua White, who co-owns Dogue Spa where Penelope gets treated, says he regularly hears complaints. 

'Owners tell us all the time that 'I don't even pay this much for my haircut,' but imagine if a person is washing you and doing your nails. It all adds up,' he said. 

Top groomers expect clients to commit to regular pricey appointments, and will strike them from their books if they refuse to pay-up consistently.

Fanny Karpman, a self-described Los Angeles 'influencer,' says she gives her labradoodle Gracie Rainbows a blowout for $400 a month.  

'If you have a doodle, it's like the black market if you can find a good doodle groomer,' she said, noting that she'll book appointments over a year in advance. 

They're what Fluffy Ruff Dog Spa of Washington State owner Julie Rust calls 'mega-doodles,' weighing over 100 pounds.

Rust tells her clients that if they can't get the dog groomed every four to six weeks, seek another spa.  

Jenniffer Walter, who runs a human beauty salon in Queens, spends $120 a month on grooming and getting a facial mask for her dog, which is named Megan Fox because 'she looks like a model.'

Other spa owners note the difficulty in keeping doodles sitting still and having to bribe them with CBD treats, broths and beef livers to keep them in place.

A Chicago lawyer, Pamela Berkowitz, said her dog groomer 'fired' them after she claimed her dog Bernice wasn't entirely shaved. 

'Maybe that's just Bernice's plight,' Ms. Berkowitz says. 'I also have curly hair, so I relate.' 

The cost of pet services rose about 6.4 percent in April compared to 2022. They outpaced the increase in cost for human haircuts (5.3 percent).
