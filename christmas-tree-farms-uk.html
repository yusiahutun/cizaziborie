10 Christmas tree farms to visit in the UK and everything you need to know before you do

Publish date: 2024-08-05

Like mince pies and mulled wine, putting up the Christmas tree is a long-held tradition that marks the start of the festive season. While artificial Christmas trees certainly have their charms, they aren’t so great for the planet. There’s also something special about selecting a real Christmas tree yourself and wrangling it home after a day out at one of the UK’s best Christmas tree farms.

There are plenty of festive farms to visit in the UK, whose perks range from modest cafes to full-blown Christmas fairs, complete with Santa’s grotto, reindeer, market stalls and mulled cider.

To get your 2024 Christmas tree adventure off to a good start, here our guide to Christmas tree farms.

What do I need to think about before I go to the Christmas tree farm?

Jo, from the The British Christmas Tree Growers’ Association (BCTGA), recommends taking measurements of your designated tree area before heading to the farm. “Work out the size of the tree you require before heading to your local grower to buy. Trees always look smaller on the farm than they will in your home.”

You should also consider the species of tree you want to buy, “a good grower will give advice on this,” Jo adds. “The trees’ location, if you have young kids that want to decorate it, how long you want the tree to last and your budget will all play into the right species of tree for you.”

How to choose the right real Christmas tree for your space

Real Christmas trees can come pre-cut (by your fair hand if you’re up for it), cut fresh by a wood cutter or potted. Think about the shape you prefer: full and bushy or slender and elegant?

Consider the needle type too, as different varieties have unique characteristics. For example, the Nordmann Fir is a popular choice as it boasts excellent needle retention.

Ever wondered what makes each variety special? Here's a quick rundown:

  • Nordmann Fir –The Nordmann Fir boasts great symmetry and has soft, non-drop needles in a rich, deep green colour, the perfect backdrop for your sparkly baubles.
  • Norway Spruce – This Christmas tree classic has a great fragrance but you’ll need to keep on top of watering to prevent it shedding its needles.
  • Blue Spruce – For something a little different, opt for this sturdy tree with a striking pale blue hue.
  • Fraser Fir – Ideal for more compact spaces, the Fraser Fir has dark green foliage with silvery-blue undertones, a pleasant scent and great needle retention. It has slightly upward facing branches – ideal dangling material for tinsel or lights.
  • Serbian Spruce – An elegant tree sporting great needle retention and dark green needles with a silvery underside laid out on thickly layered branches - perfect for the maximalist with an endless supply of decorations to hang.

When is the best time to visit a Christmas tree farm?

The UK’s Christmas tree farms start to welcome visitors in November. Aim to visit the farms on a weekday, when they’re likely to be quieter. If you’re going for a cut tree, it’s best to wait until the end of November or December if you can, otherwise your tree may start looking a bit forlorn come Christmas time. Pre-cut trees can last around four weeks.

Best Christmas tree farms to visit in the UK

FRIEZELAND CHRISTMAS TREE FARM – WARWICKSHIRE

You’ll be spoilt for choice at the UK’s largest Christmas tree farm, which supplies the likes of John Lewis and the PM’s residence with specimens from its festive fields in Warwickshire (the wreath in prime position outside Number 10 this year will come from Friezeland). You can cut your own tree or choose one that’s been freshly harvested (the less muddy option). If you need more time selecting your favourite, you can book a holiday here at one of their lakeside lodges.

WILDERNESS WOOD – EAST SUSSEX

Wilderness Wood is more than a Christmas tree farm, it’s a celebration of trees, craftsmanship and the beauty that arises when the two mix. Expect to see expertly carved buildings and projects on your wanders. Enter full festive mode at their Christmas Fayre Weekends ( 25th, 26th November and 2nd, 3rd, 9th, 10th December), when you can warm up with mulled cider and roasted chestnuts, and browse hand-made bits for those last minute gifts. For 'cut your own' trees, you'll need to reserve your tree on November 11th or 12th, from 10am to 4pm.

MORE INFO

MELDON ESTATE – NORTHUMBERLAND

At this sprawling country estate, you can cut down your own Norway Spruce, pick up a pre-cut Nordman if you’re in a hurry, or choose a planet-friendly potted one. Not to worry if you don’t want the pressure of keeping the potted tree alive until next year, you can return it to Meldon Park for safe keeping when Christmas is done and dusted and get money off next year’s purchase.

MORE INFO

CLEARWELL FARM – SOUTH EAST WALES

Those aiming for a more conscious Christmas will feel at home at Clearwell Farm, also the site of a wind turbine, solar park and a sheep-grazing Christmas tree plantation. As well as the usual varieties, Clearwell grow Grand firs, which will fill your home with a citrusy-orange scent, plus there are Christmas wreaths and an enticing array of festive foliage for decking the halls.

MORE INFO

PULL A PINE WITH THE RSPB, DORSET

Pair your Christmas tree hunt with some conservation work at one of the RSPB’s annual ‘Pull a Pine’ events. This year, the charity needs help removing invasive pine trees from protected heathland at the Arne nature reserve. In return for your graft, you get to keep the trees.

MORE INFO

HANS CHRISTMAS ANDERSON – SURREY

This experience comes complete with reindeers – and its owner used to be chairman of the British Christmas Tree Growers Association, so you know these trees are in safe hands.

MORE INFO

DARTMOOR CHRISTMAS TREE FARM – DEVON

Boasting lovely tree symmetry, it’s not hard to see why this family-run farm won Christmas Tree Grower of the Year 2023, and will be carting off one of its top specimens to Downing Street this year. While you can order online, strolling through their fragrant Christmas tree forest, surrounded by Devon countryside, is a real treat. Once you’ve found ‘the one,’ their friendly team will do the hard work of chopping it down for you, and they’ll collect it when Christmas is over for a small fee.

MORE INFO

PEACH HAYNE ORGANICS – DEVON

Christmas is traditionally a time of giving, and what better way to give back to nature than with an organic Christmas tree grown without toxic pesticides or herbicides. Peach Hayne Organics is purportedly the UK’s only certified Christmas tree farm. You can choose between Nordmann Firs and Fraser Firs of varying sizes.

MORE INFO

SOUTH LONDON CHRISTMAS TREE FARM – KENT

All the trees you see here have been nurtured from a seedling so tick the low-carbon box. You can cut your own – so long as you bring your own tools (power tools are not allowed), or leave the cutting down to the professionals. Plus, you’ll get Santa hats for your little ones.

MORE INFO

FRENCHAY FARM – BRISTOL

Bristolians can keep things local and ethical at this family-run farm, which has a raft of measures in place to ensure it attracts some wild inhabitants alongside families hunting for their perfect tree. Alongside a range of Christmas tree varieties, they’ve planted a native woodland and let wildflowers grow among the trees. They’ll recycle your tree for you at the end of the season and use the mulch on the current trees, ensuring your festive centrepiece is waste-free.

Getting your Christmas tree home

Once you’ve settled on your tree of choice, it’s time for the fun bit: getting it home! Some of the Christmas tree farms we’ve listed offer local home delivery – but if you’re planning on getting yours home yourself, bring strong twine or bungee cord to secure your tree to your car's roof or boot. Protect your car's interior and exterior with a dust sheet, as pine needles and sap can be messy.

Jo says a common mistake people make is taking the tree straight into the house. For the early birds, a word of caution: “only buy/take the tree into your home when you need it. Until then, leave it outside in a bucket of water in a shaded/sheltered area. Once you’ve brought it into your house, put it into your stand and the last thing you do is remove the protective netting.”

As you might expect, evergreen trees aren’t big fans of central heating so, if you notice the needles dropping, move it away from your radiators. And remember to water!

Are Christmas tree farms bad for the environment?

Sussing out your Christmas trees’ eco credentials isn’t as straightforward as you might think. The carbon footprint of fake plastic trees is said to be around 20 times higher than real trees. But a real tree that ends up in landfill isn’t great either, emitting some four to five times as much carbon as one that doesn’t, according to Carbon Trust figures.

Christmas trees are often grown on soil that doesn’t support other crops, and a Christmas tree farm can help prevent flood risk and attract wildlife. We suggest searching for organically grown varieties, if you’re worried about overuse of pesticides and fertiliser, or buy a potted tree you can plant out at a later date.

The British Christmas Tree Growers Association

Additionally, you can look out for the BCTGA logo. This is a sign the farm is committed to growing Christmas trees as sustainably as possible. Jo says BCTGA members take steps to “care for wildlife and the environment, which include timing mowing, cultivations, hedge cutting, crop protection and crop shearing to limit soil erosion and have minimum effect on wildlife, particularly nesting birds.”

Jo suggests also keeping the following things in mind to ensure your tree has its ethics nailed down.

“Firstly, buy directly from the farm if possible where the trees were grown. Ask plenty of questions. The growers and their staff should be able to give you guidance on these."

Example questions:

  • Where do you source your young plants from?
  • What sustainable management processes do you have in place?
  • Ask if they offer or know of any local schemes that offer tree recycling.
