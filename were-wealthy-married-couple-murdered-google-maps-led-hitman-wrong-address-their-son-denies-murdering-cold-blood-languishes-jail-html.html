Were a wealthy married couple murdered after Google maps led hitman to the wrong address? Their son

Publish date: 2024-03-14
  • Dennis and Merna Koula were found dead by their son on May 24th 2010 at home in La Crosse, Wisconsin
  • Their killings bore all the hallmarks of a professional hit
  • Investigators speculated that their killer could have made a mistake using Google Maps - their neighbor had received death threats

Were a wealthy Wisconsin couple gunned down in cold blood inside their home because the assassin was led to the wrong address by Google Maps?

That tragic twist of fate is one theory investigators in La Crosse looked at after Dennis Koula, 68 and his wife, Merna, 65, were found dead in their home by their son Eric in May of 2010 -  and who is now serving two consecutive life-sentences for their murder.

But as their son continues to deny their murder from jail, it has been revealed that just days after the Koulas were found dead a neighbor reported to police that he had received death threats, indicating the possibility a simple GPS error may have led to their murders at the hands of a contract killer.

Quickly ruling out a bungled burglary, sheriff's in the quiet, rural town were stuck by the clinical nature of the murders, 'It has all the markings of a hit, I've been told,' said Eric in an interview about the killings to CBS News on Saturday.

Police investigators examined the theory that Dennis and Merna Koula were murdered by a hitman who was directed to the wrong address by Google Maps

Discovered dead just yards from each other inside their home after being shot in the head, the Koulas son Eric raised the alarm on Monday, May 24th and authorities were immediately stumped for any motive.

John Christophersen, a special agent at the time with the Wisconsin Department of Justice, could see no reason for the killings - but did not have long to speculate.

Just days after they were found dead, one of the Koulas neighbours, Steve Burgess, a president at a local bank, came forward with startling information.

'Steve Burgess came forward to the sheriff's department, stating that he had been receiving threats,' said Christophersen to CBS News.

'And they were distinctly death threats.'

This caused the investigators to believe that a case of mistaken identity could have occurred, after they realized the hitman may have made the simple mistake of following the GPS of his computer to the wrong address.

It is difficult and confusing for any prospective assassin using Google Maps to locate the home of Dennis and Merna Koula in La Crosse, Wisconsin

'In fact, when you Google Earth, Steve Burgess' address...the zoom into the house goes to the Koula's house, not to Steve Burgess' house,' explains Peter Van Sant of CBS News.

After both failed to arrive for work on Monday morning, Eric Koula traveled to his parent's four bedroom home and entered to find his dad lying on the kitchen floor, face down in a pool of blood.

Desperately phoning 911, he walked into the computer room to find his mother, Merna, slumped over the computer, with her left arm still on the keypad, her right arm resting on her right knee.

'She died instantaneously and she did not know what was coming,' said Christophersen.

What struck officials in the immediate aftermath of the brutal shootings was that so many valuables have been left untouched.

Merna worked as a part-time teacher in the wealthy town and her husband Dennis made his millions from a chain of pharmacies and a Ford car dealership that he owned in La Crosse

'I see electronics, I see a wallet that's undisturbed, I see a purse that is undisturbed, a computer sitting out,' said Christophersen.

'I see credit cars and cash in closets and in dresser drawers that are undisturbed. I see jewelry boxes that haven't been touched.

'An individual who's going to burglarize a place is going to open up the top drawer, shuffle through it, close the drawer and them look into the second drawer.

'If you look they're indicating that they go to the bottom drawer first, pull it out, shuffle through it then pull out the next drawer and so forth going up.'

A crucial point in the investigation came when they realized that they could use Merna's last touch on the keyboard would reveal her exact time of death.

'We had a forsensic computer expert take a look at the computer that Merna was on, and her last keystroke on that computer was Friday, 5.41 p.m. on May 21st,' said Christophersen.

That led police to know that the couple had been dead for three days before Eric found them - a piece of information they kept secret during their initial investigations.

'So no matter what timeline individuals provide to us at this point for their alibi, we now have a precise time that we can relate to as to whether or not they could've committed the crime,' said Christophersen.

Eric Koula discovered his dead parents on the morning of Monday 21st May 2010 and his sister Cindy (right) was devastated

Christophersen says they were both shot once with a .22 caliber rifle. Investigators began chasing the first lead -- the death threats to that banker who lived up the road. However, they found the culprit who made the calls, but ruled him out.

'The individual that we believe was responsible for those threats would not have been able to carry out the homicides, because they had a legitimate alibi at the time that Dennis and Merna were killed,' he explained.

With nothing to go on - law enforcement officials began to examine the most likely suspects in any killing - close friends and family.

As it happened, Eric celebrated his 16th wedding anniversary on the evening of his parents death and he provided police with an alibi.

His sister Cindy provided police with a work time-stamp to prove where she was.

However, just four days after the Koulas bodies were found, Eric discovered a shocking note that read only 'Fixed You' in his mailbox.

The town of La Crosse rarely experiences crime - let alone a double homicide with no forcible entry

'I went out to get the mail. Opened the mailbox, brought the note back in,' said Eric.

The investigation into the Koulas deaths now took on a new urgency. Deputies examined the bank records of all the family members and found Eric's account had seen a very large deposit.

'On Saturday, after his parents were killed, he had deposited a $50,000 check into his account,' said Christophersen.

The check was signed by his father Dennis Koula and Eric had failed to inform investigators.

'And I know it looked terrible. I had gotten this check from my dad several days ago. I went and cashed it on Saturday,' said Eric.

'And now they're dead. And my dad, you know, was the one guy that can back me up on this. And he's not here to do that. You know, I knew I'd have to eventually explain the check and talk about it. And I could. And I would.'

Now focusing their attention onto Eric, police wondered what else he wasn't telling them.

'It doesn't stand out at first, but once you take the time to listen to the 911 call, you realize that Eric never asks for help for his parents,' said Christophersen.

However, the biggest eye-opener in the case was unveiled when police discover that Eric was flat broke - providing them with a motive.

Over the course of the previous three years, Eric's father had provided him with nearly one million dollars to fund his failed career as a day trader - and he now was $150,000 in debt.

Re-examining every inch of his alibi, Christophersen found that Eric did not appear on any of the security cameras at the store where he said he was buying a potted plant for his wife at the time of the shootings.

Dennis and Eric Koula were considered to be like best friends - Dennis had bank rolled his son to the tune of almost $1 million before his death

While he claims to have been at the store at 5.41 p.m., no trace of him in the garden center can be found at that time - and his receipt from that day reads 6.15 p.m.

Investigators believe that gave him a window of opportunity to go to his parents house and kill them and return to the store to make the purchase.

Over the course of the next two months, a case was quietly built up against Eric.

A handwriting expert even determined that the signature on the $50,000 was a forgery and Eric was arrested on two counts of first-degree intentional homicide.

So, in June 2012, La Crosse County District Attorney Tim Gruenke opened the case against Eric Koula.

'And the truth is, the defendant is guilty of murdering his parents,' said Gruenke, as he addressed the court.

'We want to lay out for the jury that Eric was a man of secrets. He's not who he appears to be. He's somebody who has a desperate financial motive for the crime.'

Indeed, during the trial, Eric was forced to admit he did, in fact, sign his father's name onto the $50,00 check.

'I've signed my dad's signature on hundreds and hundreds of checks, from personal, paying bills at the house, to at the store when we had double signature checks, hundreds and hundreds of times,' claimed Eric.

Despite no DNA evidence linking Eric with the murder that day, the circumstantial trial took another twist when Eric admited he wrote the 'Fixed You' note.

Eric Koula became the prime suspect in the murder of his parents - with his financial insolvency being the motive

'I wrote the note. And I put it in the mailbox,' said Eric.

Eric claims he planted the note after investigators questioned his 16-year-old son, Dexter during their inquiries.

'And I just wanted them to leave me and my family alone, and get who killed my mom and dad. I know what I did was really stupid. And I'm so sorry I did it,' Eric told the court.

The defense contended that the fact that Eric deposited the $50,000 the day after the murders is proof of his innocence.

'What sort of an idiot would put a check in the bank the morning after they killed their parents, knowing that bank records are easy to get?' said attorney Keith Belzer.

Eric Koula was sentenced to two consecutive life sentences with no chance of parole in August 2012 for the murder of his parents in May 2010

After 20 hours of deliberations the jury found Eric Koula guilty of murder.

'And my heart just stopped for a second there,' said his sister Cindy of the verdict.

'It's like, 'Wow!' Eight-million thoughts through your head. 'Mom and Dad are dead. Their killer's going to prison, was found guilty. Wait a second. That killer's my brother.'

Judge Scott Horne handed Eric two consecutive life sentences without possibility of parole in August of last year.

'You took the life of the two people who gave you life. And you'll spend the rest of your life incarcerated.'
